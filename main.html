Introduction

After the booming phase driven by the Industrial Revolution in the 18th century, endless outward urban expansion reached the limit, how to sustainably manage and develop cities turned into an issue. To address that, urban regeneration theory was introduced, aimed at restoring socio-economic vitality of designated urban areas by replanning and reconstructing.

The expansion of the transport network is a common means to unlock urban renewal in contemporary times. Most successful regeneration relies on a good public transport system, which changes existing population distribution patterns and consumption characteristics through increased urban mobility, thereby driving redevelopment of the dilapidated areas.

This work proposes to understand the impact of transport as a strategy for urban renewal, taking the two opportunity areas â€“ the Docklands and Stratford as examples, to discuss how Jubilee Line Extension has pushed the East London urban regeneration process.

Jubilee Line Extension and East London

The Jubilee Line Extension (JLE) is the extension of the London Underground Jubilee line from Green Park to Stratford, construction began in 1993 and opened in December 1999. The extension interchanges with 7 other tube lines, the docklands light railway(DLR) and 6 National Rail stations, bringing tremendous spatial changes and socio-economic benefits to East London.

  • Before 2000

    East London declined rapidly in the post-war period in the mid-20th century. The rise of aviation and increasing shipping size led to the rundown of London Dockland, East London heavily suffered from economic recession and population loss. As a consequence, London Docklands Development Corporation(LDDC) was established in the 1980s to revitalise the depressed Dockland areas, Jubilee Line Extension is part of the regeneration project.

  • 2001 - 2012

    The opening of JEL greatly improved the accessibility of East London, strengthened the inter-connection between East and Central. The success of 2012 London olympic bidding was inseparable from the upgraded transportation network. A series of frameworks and strategies highlighted the redevelopment opportunities of East London around Olympic Legacy, through policy guiding further boosting the competitiveness and attractiveness of the area. The 2012 London Olympic is considered as a successful and typical sports-led regeneration.

Case Studies

1. Urban Transformation

The Visualization of Two Opportunity Areas in East London

The Docklands:

The Docklands has faced the loss of jobs caused by containerisation (Hobhouse, 1994), which led to economic decline, social issues, and limited investment in the area. Key players in the dockland regeneration included the London Docklands Development Corporation, which attracted investments such as HSBC; the Central Government, responsible for planning and granting permissions; Transport for London (TFL), which implemented the DLR and Jubilee line links to the Docklands; and Crossrail, involved in building a new transport hub connecting both ends of London by 2020. The Docklands regeneration aimed to address these challenges and revitalise the docklands for sustainable development.


Stratford:

The urban regeneration process in Stratford, a deprived area north of the Docklands in London, has undergone different stages. Stratford's selection as a host for the 2012 Olympic and Paralympic Games was driven by its high unemployment and poor infrastructure. The successful bid aimed to use Stratford during the games and regenerate it as the Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park from 2008 to 2011. After 2012, the focus shifted to more technical-based innovation areas such as the East Bank project, International Quarter London, and subsequent international sporting events.
